To be precise, we'll consider its Return On Capital Employed (ROCE), as that will inform our view of the quality of the business.First up, we'll look at what ROCE is and how we calculate it. Next, we'll compare it to others in its industry. Then we'll determine how its current liabilities are affecting its ROCE.What is Return On Capital Employed (ROCE)?ROCE is a measure of a company's yearly pre-tax profit (its return), relative to the capital employed in the business. All else being equal, a better business will have a higher ROCE. Ultimately, it is a useful but imperfect metric. Renowned investment researcher Michael Mauboussin has suggested that a high ROCE can indicate that 'one dollar invested in the company generates value of more than one dollar'.How Do You Calculate Return On Capital Employed?Analysts use this formula to calculate return on capital employed:Return on Capital Employed = Earnings Before Interest and Tax (EBIT) ÷ (Total Assets - Current Liabilities)Or for Gérard Perrier Industrie:0.20 = €19m ÷ (€159m - €66m) (Based on the trailing twelve months to June 2019.)So, Gérard Perrier Industrie has an ROCE of 20%.

Using our data, we find that Gérard Perrier Industrie's ROCE is meaningfully better than the 12% average in the Electrical industry. We consider this a positive sign, because it suggests it uses capital more efficiently than similar companies. Setting aside the comparison to its industry for a moment, Gérard Perrier Industrie's ROCE in absolute terms currently looks quite high.The image below shows how Gérard Perrier Industrie's ROCE compares to its industry, and you can click it to see more detail on its past growth.ENXTPA:PERR Past Revenue and Net Income, January 8th 2020MoreWhen considering this metric, keep in mind that it is backwards looking, and not necessarily predictive. Companies in cyclical industries can be difficult to understand using ROCE, as returns typically look high during boom times, and low during busts. This is because ROCE only looks at one year, instead of considering returns across a whole cycle. The ROCE equation subtracts current liabilities from capital employed, so a company with a lot of current liabilities appears to have less capital employed, and a higher ROCE than otherwise. To counteract this, we check if a company has high current liabilities, relative to its total assets.Gérard Perrier Industrie has total liabilities of €66m and total assets of €159m. As a result, its current liabilities are equal to approximately 41% of its total assets. Gérard Perrier Industrie's ROCE is boosted somewhat by its middling amount of current liabilities.What We Can Learn From Gérard Perrier Industrie's ROCEStory continuesEven so, it has a great ROCE, and could be an attractive prospect for further research.
