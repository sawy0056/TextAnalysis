See our latest analysis for Orell Füssli Holding How Does Martin Buyle's Compensation Compare With Similar Sized Companies?At the time of writing, our data says that Orell Füssli Holding AG has a market cap of CHF210m, and reported total annual CEO compensation of CHF776k for the year to December 2018. While this analysis focuses on total compensation, it's worth noting the salary is lower, valued at CHF370k. As part of our analysis we looked at companies in the same jurisdiction, with market capitalizations of CHF97m to CHF389m. The median total CEO compensation was CHF803k.That means Martin Buyle receives fairly typical remuneration for the CEO of a company that size. Although this fact alone doesn't tell us a great deal, it becomes more relevant when considered against the business performance.You can see, below, how CEO compensation at Orell Füssli Holding has changed over time.SWX:OFN CEO Compensation, January 10th 2020MoreIs Orell Füssli Holding AG Growing?On average over the last three years, Orell Füssli Holding AG has shrunk earnings per share by 121% each year (measured with a line of best fit). Its revenue is down 11% over last year.Few shareholders would be pleased to read that earnings per share are lower over three years. And the fact that revenue is down year on year arguably paints an ugly picture. These factors suggest that the business performance wouldn't really justify a high pay packet for the CEO. Shareholders might be interested in this free visualization of analyst forecasts.Has Orell Füssli Holding AG Been A Good Investment?Given the total loss of 3.2% over three years, many shareholders in Orell Füssli Holding AG are probably rather dissatisfied, to say the least. This suggests it would be unwise for the company to pay the CEO too generously.In Summary...Remuneration for Martin Buyle is close enough to the median pay for a CEO of a similar sized company .After looking at EPS and total shareholder returns, it's certainly hard to argue the company has performed well, since both metrics are down. Few would argue that it's wise for the company to pay any more, before returns improve.
