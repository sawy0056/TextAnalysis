To be precise, we'll consider its Return On Capital Employed (ROCE), as that will inform our view of the quality of the business.First of all, we'll work out how to calculate ROCE. Then we'll compare its ROCE to similar companies. Finally, we'll look at how its current liabilities affect its ROCE.Return On Capital Employed (ROCE): What is it?ROCE measures the amount of pre-tax profits a company can generate from the capital employed in its business. Generally speaking a higher ROCE is better. In brief, it is a useful tool, but it is not without drawbacks. Renowned investment researcher Michael Mauboussin has suggested that a high ROCE can indicate that 'one dollar invested in the company generates value of more than one dollar'.How Do You Calculate Return On Capital Employed?Analysts use this formula to calculate return on capital employed:Return on Capital Employed = Earnings Before Interest and Tax (EBIT) ÷ (Total Assets - Current Liabilities)Or for Fluidra:0.031 = €83m ÷ (€3.0b - €370m) (Based on the trailing twelve months to September 2019.)So, Fluidra has an ROCE of 3.1%.
See our latest analysis for Fluidra Is Fluidra's ROCE Good?When making comparisons between similar businesses, investors may find ROCE useful. We can see Fluidra's ROCE is meaningfully below the Machinery industry average of 8.4%. This performance is not ideal, as it suggests the company may not be deploying its capital as effectively as some competitors. Independently of how Fluidra compares to its industry, its ROCE in absolute terms is low; especially compared to the ~0.4% available in government bonds. It is likely that there are more attractive prospects out there.Fluidra's current ROCE of 3.1% is lower than its ROCE in the past, which was 8.0%, 3 years ago. Therefore we wonder if the company is facing new headwinds. The image below shows how Fluidra's ROCE compares to its industry, and you can click it to see more detail on its past growth.BME:FDR Past Revenue and Net Income, January 3rd 2020MoreIt is important to remember that ROCE shows past performance, and is not necessarily predictive. Companies in cyclical industries can be difficult to understand using ROCE, as returns typically look high during boom times, and low during busts. ROCE is, after all, simply a snap shot of a single year. What happens in the future is pretty important for investors, so we have prepared a free report on analyst forecasts for Fluidra.What Are Current Liabilities, And How Do They Affect Fluidra's ROCE?Short term (or current) liabilities, are things like supplier invoices, overdrafts, or tax bills that need to be paid within 12 months. The ROCE equation subtracts current liabilities from capital employed, so a company with a lot of current liabilities appears to have less capital employed, and a higher ROCE than otherwise. To counteract this, we check if a company has high current liabilities, relative to its total assets.Fluidra has total assets of €3.0b and current liabilities of €370m. As a result, its current liabilities are equal to approximately 12% of its total assets. With a very reasonable level of current liabilities, so the impact on ROCE is fairly minimal.What We Can Learn From Fluidra's ROCEStory continuesWhile that is good to see, Fluidra has a low ROCE and does not look attractive in this analysis.
